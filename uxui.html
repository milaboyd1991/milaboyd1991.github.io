What this case study is for.

This is not a gallery of screens. It is a working academic document built to Professor Pak's creative brief template, expanded with two meta layers a final-year student should own: a growth narrative comparing who I was in 2022 to who I am now, and a working framework for choosing between manual, AI-powered, and hybrid design workflows. The anchor artifact is the PNW Floor Design mobile app — a real product for my real flooring company in Washington, designed using the design thinking process and the Bootstrap component skills from Advanced UX.

Growth Narrative

Who I was, who I am now.

In January 2022 Professor Pak wrote a letter of recommendation describing me as "naturally talented and hardworking," with "an incredibly astute attention to detail" and a "methodical creative process." That was the student version of me: careful, observant, willing to refine a single composition until it locked.

Four years and a return to school later, the careful attention is still there. What is new is the system thinking. I no longer try to make one beautiful artifact at a time. I build the rules the artifacts come from.

Then · 2022
  • One artifact at a time. Polish, refine, ship.
  • Composition-first. The page is the unit of work.
  • Colors and type chosen project by project.
  • "Does this poster look good?"
  • Tools as means to an end.
Now · 2026
  • System-first. Rules generate the artifacts.
  • The token (color, spacing, weight) is the unit of work.
  • One design system inherited across every page.
  • "Does this decision compound across the product?"
  • Tools as collaborators, weighed against the task.
"Critical thinking is your strongest skill. Build custom design systems to stand out from generic UI components. Strong case studies are the most important strategy."

Working Framework

Manual, AI-powered, or hybrid?

The honest question every working designer faces in 2026 is no longer whether to use AI. It is when. The framework I work from is simple: do not let AI make a decision that defines the brand or the user. Let AI carry the volume so the designer can hold the judgment.

Manual

When the human owns it.

  • Design system tokens (color, type, spacing)
  • Information architecture and user flow
  • Brand voice and tone of every page
  • Critical UX decisions (privacy, accessibility, hierarchy)
  • Final visual refinement

Why manual: these are the choices that compound across every screen. A bad token poisons the whole product.

AI-Powered

When volume is the bottleneck.

  • Hero image exploration and moodboards
  • First-draft copy, then humanized
  • Boilerplate HTML and repetitive markup
  • Pattern checks (accessibility, readability)
  • Batch file operations and compression

Why AI: tasks where doing it by hand costs more attention than the work is worth. AI does the boring so the human can do the brave.

Hybrid · the strongest mode

When AI proposes, the designer judges.

  • AI generates five options, designer picks one and refines
  • AI drafts the code, designer corrects to the system
  • AI catches vibe-coded tells, designer rewrites in voice
  • AI handles file plumbing, designer protects the asset library
  • Always: the designer holds the brand and the system

Why hybrid: a designer with a clear system can use AI without losing themselves in it. A designer without a system gets eaten by it.

The rule I keep: if a decision can be reversed cheaply, AI can take the first pass. If a decision shapes everything downstream, the human takes the first pass.

Creative Brief · Pak's Template

PNW Floor Design app, the full brief.

Following Professor Pak's eight-section creative brief template, exactly as taught in DDSGN 151. The PNW Floor Design app is the synthesis of ten years inside the flooring industry and four years studying UX/UI.

4.1 · Background

Where this came from.

PNW Floor Design LLC was created from resilience, creativity, business strategy, and a vision to modernize how flooring companies connect with people. I worked directly with customers inside the flooring industry for over ten years — helping homeowners select materials, solve layout problems, understand product performance, and create spaces that feel aligned with their lifestyle.

Over time, I noticed the same gap on every consultation. Most companies focus only on selling product. Very few design for customer experience, digital systems, visual organization, or project transparency. I saw confusion. I saw homeowners overwhelmed. I saw businesses struggling with their own workflows. I realized I could help bridge that gap, but to do it well I needed UX/UI training. That decision is what brought me back to Pierce College in August 2024.

4.2 · Project Objectives

Problems and goals.

Problem statement. Homeowners struggle to plan renovation projects because they cannot visualize materials, understand the steps, or manage communication with their flooring vendor.

Goals.

  • Give homeowners a way to preview materials in their own space before they commit money.
  • Make booking a consultation a one-tap action, not a phone call.
  • Show every project phase in a single live timeline so customers know what is next.
  • Keep chat with the designer inside the app so messages do not get lost across email and text.
  • Integrate heated-floor smart control so the product extends past install day.
4.3 · Key Message

What the brand says.

Beauty. Quality. Lasting impressions. Flooring that transforms every step.

Most flooring companies sell product. PNW Floor Design sells the experience around it. The app turns a high-anxiety purchase into a guided, transparent process where the homeowner stays in control from material selection through final install.

4.4 · Target Audience

Who this is for.

Two real personas synthesized from ten years of showroom conversations.

Persona 01
The Working Parent

Age: 42 · Location: Tacoma, WA

Role: Working parent, second home renovation

Goal: A flooring contractor who keeps her informed without phone calls. No time for showroom visits between work and pickup.

Frustration: The last contractor disappeared after the contract signed. She had to chase status updates.

Needs from PNW app: Project timeline, in-app chat, push notifications for delivery and install dates.

Persona 02
The First-Time Homeowner

Age: 31 · Location: Seattle, WA

Role: First-time homeowner, never picked flooring before

Goal: Understand what the categories mean (LVP vs. engineered hardwood vs. tile) without sounding ignorant to the designer.

Frustration: Houzz is inspiring but overwhelming. Floor & Decor's site does not explain enough. Home Depot is product-first, not design-first.

Needs from PNW app: Material Library with plain-language descriptions, filters by use-case (bathroom, kitchen, basement), saved favorites.

4.6 · Solutions & Requirements

What the app must do.

  • Empathy-driven research first. The app starts from real customer pain, not a feature list.
  • Task-based interface. Sections are organized by what a homeowner is trying to do (browse materials, book a call, track a project) — not by what a flooring company sells.
  • Plain language. Product copy is written for a first-time homeowner. No jargon that requires a footnote.
  • Project transparency by default. Every project phase visible by default. The homeowner does not have to ask.
  • Trend-aware. AR visualization, AI material recommendations, smart-home integration, self-service tools — designed in from the start, not bolted on later.

01 · Research

Ten years of real showroom conversations turned into a research foundation. Documented the most common homeowner pain points: not remembering samples, losing communication after contract signed, confusion about material categories.

Mode: Manual. Real notes, real customers.

02 · Ideation · Empathy Map + Journey Map

Synthesized findings into two anonymous personas (The Working Parent, The First-Time Homeowner) and a customer journey: discover → consult → select → quote → install → live with. Identified post-contract communication as the steepest emotional drop.

Mode: Hybrid. AI helped cluster interview themes. Persona writing was mine.

03 · Low Fidelity · Sketches

Hand sketches and Figma low-fi wireframes for the ten core screens. Focus on flow, not pixels. Confirmed the navigation pattern (bottom-tab) and the priority of Material Library, Project Timeline, Chat.

Mode: Manual. The structure had to be mine.

04 · High Fidelity · Figma

Locked the visual design system (lavender, paper, dark-green primary, soft pink secondary, SF Pro + Source Serif Pro). Built component library first. Screens compounded instead of fighting each other.

Mode: Hybrid. AI helped with first-pass component variants. I refined every screen.

05 · Case Study Document

A 60-page case study PDF documenting every screen, every flow, the persona reasoning, and the visual system. Originally 153 MB. Recompressed to 14 MB without losing readability.

Mode: Hybrid. Manual writing, AI-powered compression.

06 · Final Visual Design

Final pass on every screen against the design system rules: limited palette, two-font pairing, single type scale, two radius values, subtle hover only. Stripped anything that drifted.

Mode: Manual. The system is the editor. I am the system.

Anchor Features · Deep Dive

What the app actually does.

Material Library

Problem. Homeowners cannot remember which sample they liked. Photos in a phone gallery do not preserve product code, square footage, or installation type.

Feature. Saved materials with full metadata. Filter by room type, water resistance, foot traffic, budget.

Why it matters. Removes the "I think it was the gray one" conversation. The sample stays with the customer.

Project Timeline

Problem. After the contract signs, communication drops off. Homeowners do not know when materials arrive, when install starts, or what is next.

Feature. Live timeline of every project phase. Push notifications on phase transitions.

Why it matters. Project visibility is trust. Trust is repeat business.

Heated Floor Control

Problem. Smart-home control of radiant heat is fragmented across vendor apps. No one app knows the room layout the floor was installed under.

Feature. IoT integration mapped to the original install plan. Schedule by zone, by day, by occupancy.

Why it matters. The flooring product becomes the platform, not just the floor.

Design System Connection

The portfolio site holds the same rules.

The PNW Floor Design app uses lavender luxury tokens. This portfolio site uses warm sepia tokens. Different brands, same discipline. The five constraints below apply to every project I touch.

5colors max
2fonts max
3type sizes
2radius values
3pxmax hover lift

Five colors means I cannot hide behind variety. Two fonts means the type pairing carries the emotion, not a third font. Three sizes means the hierarchy is decided once. Two radius values means every corner agrees. A 3-pixel maximum hover lift means the interaction is felt, not announced. Restraint is the proof that the designer made a choice.

Discoveries

What four years actually taught me.

A design system makes me smarter than I am alone.

When I have a system, I can think about the user instead of the pixels. The system holds the pixels for me. When I do not have a system, every decision exhausts a budget I cannot afford to spend.

AI is a microphone, not a voice.

AI can make my voice louder, faster, and reach further. It cannot replace the voice. The moment I let AI hold the brand voice, the brand stops being mine. The discipline is to ask AI for the volume work and never the identity work.

Restraint reads as class.

The 500-site audit on vibe-coded portfolios named the same patterns I had been making: purple gradients, em-dashes, glowing hovers, fake testimonials, placeholder names. None of those patterns are bad in isolation. All of them together signal a missing system. I removed every one of them from this site on purpose.

The flooring industry taught me product design before I had the word for it.

Ten years of helping homeowners pick tile, calm their anxiety, manage delivery problems, and protect their budget was a product design education in disguise. UX/UI school gave me the vocabulary. The job gave me the empathy. The combination is the work.

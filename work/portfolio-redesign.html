Portfolio Redesign Journey. From academic project to professional studio platform.

Seven phases. Twenty pages. One system. The website you are reading was rebuilt from an academic portfolio into a studio platform across seven commits, and this is the record of why each one was made.

Role
Designer, Developer, Author
Type
Personal · Studio Platform
Stack
HTML, CSS, JSON-LD, GitHub Pages
Year
2026
Context

What was happening.

The original site was an academic project. It looked like an academic project. That was what needed to change.

The challenge was not creating a new website from scratch. The challenge was transforming a university-style portfolio into a professional studio platform that could support interior design, flooring, UX/UI, service design, and consulting work under one cohesive identity.

Decision Log

How the project actually moved.

Decision 01

Rebuild the information architecture as a studio, not a school.

Context

The existing site closely reflected the structure and visual language of an academic portfolio. It communicated coursework well, but it did not communicate a mature professional identity.

Constraint

The logo and core identity needed to remain recognizable, but the overall experience could not feel like a university project or a student portfolio.

Decision

I rebuilt the information architecture around a studio model rather than an academic model. Separate portfolio categories were merged into a unified Work section, academic framing was removed, and the site was reorganized around services, expertise, and case studies.

Outcome

The website shifted from a collection of projects into a professional platform capable of supporting multiple practice areas and attracting clients, collaborators, and hiring managers. Interior design, UX/UI, service design, and customer experience now share one URL without competing for it.

Reflection

People rarely hire a designer because of how many projects they completed. They hire a designer because they understand what that designer does and why it matters.

Decision 02

Build a design system that removes options instead of adding them.

Context

The original visual system carried a strong academic influence and no longer matched the tone of the practice.

Constraint

Feminine design tends to drift toward the decorative. Premium design tends to drift toward the cold. The system had to land between them and stay there.

Decision

A new design system was created using a limited palette of Forest Green, Warm Cream, Dusty Rose, Muted Gold, and Espresso Dark. Typography was simplified to Cormorant Garamond and Inter, creating consistency across all pages.

Outcome

The visual identity became more cohesive, more mature, and more aligned with the values of craftsmanship, clarity, and thoughtful decision-making. Five colors. Two fonts. Three type sizes. The system itself became evidence of the rule it teaches.

Reflection

A visual system becomes stronger when it removes options rather than adding them. Constraints create consistency.

Decision 03

Expand the project beyond visual design into authority infrastructure.

Context

The first version of the redesign focused heavily on structure, navigation, and page creation.

Constraint

A beautiful website without authority signals would still struggle to earn trust from search engines, clients, and hiring managers.

Decision

The project expanded beyond visual design to include SEO foundations, structured data, sitemap generation, social metadata, credibility systems, service positioning, and lead-generation infrastructure.

Outcome

The website became discoverable, searchable, and capable of supporting real business goals instead of functioning only as a presentation layer. Structured data across eighteen pages. JSON-LD for Person, Organization, ProfessionalService, and CreativeWork. The site became legible to hiring managers and search engines at the same time.

Reflection

A website is not the product. It is the container. The value comes from the work, the stories, and the decisions documented inside it.

Decision 04

Refactor the case study template to demand judgment, not just narration.

Context

By the final phase, the structural work was complete. What remained was the question of what the site would actually publish, and how.

Constraint

A generic Problem, Solution, Result template would let future case studies drift back into documenting work instead of demonstrating judgment. The difference between those two outcomes is the difference between a portfolio and a practice.

Decision

The case study template was refactored to use a Constraint to Decision to Outcome backbone with a mandatory Reflection step at the end of every decision block. Every future case study has to answer the same five questions, every time.

Outcome

The case study you are reading is the first one written using it. The framework is now its own evidence.

Reflection

A case study template that demands judgment from its writer is the only kind worth keeping.

Synthesis

What changed, and why.

Across every phase of the redesign, the same question appeared repeatedly: What should remain, and what should change?

The answer was rarely aesthetic. Most decisions were about clarity, positioning, and trust. The result is a studio website designed to support real work, document decisions, and grow alongside a multidisciplinary practice that spans physical and digital experiences.

A website is not the project. The work documented inside it is.
